Amphipsalta - Clapping Cicadas

A general rule of thumb for all Amphipsalta is their calls. Amphipsalta cicadas are the loudest, largest and most common of the cicadas we have in New Zealand, and, unlike our other species, can be heard by almost anyone with regular hearing. There are a total of three recognised species in New Zealand, all endemic.
They get their common name, "Clapping Cicadas" due to how they clap their wings as part of how they attract females, characterised by a loud "pop".
They prefer to sing high up, primarily on trees, and come out each summer. Ocelli [1] are normally ruby red.

Closer Look

Amphipsalta generally have a body length longer than 19mm [2] . There is a small bend in the costa [3] of their forewings [4] , with their R crossveins [5] coloured in. All three species have black patterning, and a vertical, usually yellow line on their pronotum [6] , surrounded by aformentioned black patterning. The collar of this genera's pronotum is extended outward laterally. [7]

Species Descriptions

Amphipsalta cingulata - Clapping Cicada

Clapping cicadas are easily mistaken for chorus cicadas. They're much darker in colouration than chorus cicadas, and normally take on more blacks, browns and yellows, though greens are not entirely absent. They have symmetrical black patterning along their bodies. Their underside is commonly a lighter brown with some black markings. [9]
As nymphs, Amphipsalta cingulata shells are a lighter colour than Amphipsalta zelandica shells. They are a tan, sandy colour.
The wingspan of Amphipsalta cingulata usually measure over 60mm. The forewing costa are usually olive green, with the pterostigma [10] black. the forewing R crossveins are coloured a thick black, with the hindwings' anal wing veins [11] having a fading kind of gradient as it progresses upward. The basal [12] connection of the forewing is grey.
The space between the ocelli is usually more of a closed circle compared to that of Amphipsalta zelandica, and the top surface of the frons [13] is rough and dull, with visible hairs. there is a long, centred groove that travels almost along the entire frons underside.
Amphipsalta cingulata aren't picky on where they sit, though generally stick to the lowlands. This species is distributed around the North Island, where it is very common.

Colour Morphs

There seems to be a large variety of browns, yellows, and greens clapping cicadas can take. Many of them have more black areas than other cicadas in this genus, but this is also variable. Compound eye colouration in clapping cicadas is regularly black or brown, but some individuals have remarkably bright red eyes.

Amphipsalta strepitans - Chirping Cicada - Disco-Beat Cicada

Amphipsalta strepitans is the rarer of the three species in the genus, not much research has gone into it. Chirping cicadas are also the most unobserved of all Amphipsalta species, making research annoyingly difficult.
Chirping cicadas seem to mainly come in browns, with black taking up much of the space. They have an ashy hue to their colouration.
The major, easiest way to identify this species is by its wing length. While both Amphipsalta cingulata and Amphipsalta zelandica have wingspans that reach over 60mm, Amphipsalta strepitans usually have a much smaller wingspan, under 60mm. The basal membrane of the forewing is an orange colour. Chirping cicadas also lack the gradient fade on the anal hindwing veins, unlike its two siblings.
Like the Clapping Cicada, this species has a rough and dull frons, with visible hairs. there is a long, centred groove that travels almost along the entire frons underside. The front angle of the pronotum is at a sharp recline, with a distinct point. [16]
The body is much smaller than other members of the genus, with adults reaching around 19mm.

Colour Morphs

Chirping cicadas are regularly brown and/or yellow, with green around their frons and forelegs. They've got an olive-grey tinge, sometimes look hairy, and have grey, black, or dark brown eyes.

Amphipsalta zelandica - Chorus Cicada - Kihikihi Wawā

Chorus cicadas are the most prominent, largest, and well-known cicadas in New Zealand. They are associated with the summer, their calls loud. Their bodies are frequently a nice, vibrant shade of green, surrounded by black patterning. The green fades into a yellow-brown in dead specimens. The underside is black, or sometimes has pale yellowish markings.
As nymphs, Amphipsalta zelandica are a dark, reddish brown.
The wingspan of Amphipsalta zelandica measures over 60mm. The forewing costa share a colour with the body of the cicada, with the pterostigma black or brown. the forewing R crossveins are coloured a thick black, with the hindwings' anal wing veins having a fading kind of gradient as it progresses upward. The forewing anal veins are a bright red or brown. The basal connection of the forewing is a bright blue, described as "malachite green", which is a reliable means of differentiating between this species and Amphipsalta cingulata.
The space between the ocelli opens up to seemingly connect to the vertical yellow line along the cicada's pronotum, and the top surface of the frons is smooth, shiny, and almost entirely hairless. there is a short, centred groove that travels along the frons underside, though it does not travel along the entire length like the other two species.

Colour Morphs

Chorus cicadas are regularly their standard bright green with yellow fades around the edges, where the colour meets their black patterns, but on rare occasions, they can be bright yellow. Some greens also almost appear blue, and even rarer, they are blue, with blue eyes to match.
Compound eye colouration in chorus cicadas is regularly black or brown, but may also be similar or the same green as their bodies, or a brighter red colour. The three ocelli are usually ruby red.
